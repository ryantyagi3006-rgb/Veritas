The Three Teams

Positive

For the Motion

You argue in favour of the motion. Your job is to show why the idea being debated is a good thing.

  • Build strong, evidence-based arguments
  • Challenge the negative team's claims
  • Show real-world examples that support you
Negative

Against the Motion

You argue against the motion. Your job is to show why the idea is flawed, harmful, or simply doesn't work.

  • Point out problems and limitations
  • Counter the positive team's evidence
  • Offer alternative perspectives
Collaboration

Finding Solutions

You try to bridge both sides and find real, practical solutions that everyone can agree on.

  • Listen carefully to both teams
  • Find common ground
  • Propose creative, fair solutions
2

How a Debate Works

1
Opening

Opening Statements

Each team presents their main argument. State your position clearly and give your strongest points first. Think of this as your "opening pitch."

2
Rebuttal

Crossfire & Cross-Questioning

Teams ask each other questions to challenge their arguments. Try to find weaknesses in what the other side said. Stay respectful — no interrupting!

3
⭐ Key Stage

Solution Minute

Each team presents practical, fair solutions related to the motion. This is where all three teams come together to solve the problem. One team member speaks on behalf of the group.

4
Closing

Chair's Vote

The Chair (judge) votes on the best solution presented. The most practical, fair, and clear solution wins!

3

The Solution Minute

What is the Solution Minute?

The Solution Minute happens at the very end of the debate. After all the cross-questioning is done, every team must propose real, practical answers to the problem. Here's how to nail it:

  1. 1
    Be Practical — Your solution must actually be doable in the real world. Not just a wish!
  2. 2
    Be Specific — Vague ideas don't win. Name exactly WHO should do WHAT and HOW.
  3. 3
    Be Just — Your solution must be fair to everyone affected. Think about all sides.
  4. 4
    Connect to Your Team — Positive, Negative, and Collaboration teams each present solutions that relate to their position in the debate.
  5. 5
    One Speaker Only — Choose ONE person from your team to present. They speak directly to the Chair.

Your Debate Topic: UN Peacekeeping

The Motion: "This House Believes that UN Peacekeeping Missions are Effective at Creating Lasting Peace."

🌍

Case Study: The Mano River Basin (West Africa)

A success story — 25 years of peacekeeping

25
Years of peacekeeping (1993–2018)
45K
Peak UN peacekeepers deployed
250K
Armed combatants disarmed
3.5M
Displaced people returned home
  • Brutal civil wars tore through Liberia, Sierra Leone, and Côte d'Ivoire from the mid-1990s to early 2000s, threatening to destabilize all of West Africa.
  • Six UN peacekeeping missions were deployed over 25 years. At their peak, over 45,000 peacekeepers were on the ground.
  • Over 75,000 weapons were secured and millions of rounds of ammunition were removed from circulation.
  • Nine presidential and legislative elections were supported — helping these countries become democracies.
  • War criminals were brought to justice: Liberia's Charles Taylor received a 50-year prison sentence for war crimes.
  • By 2018, the UN mission ended successfully, leaving the region at peace — a major achievement.
⚖️

Case Study: Syria (2012–2018)

Where UN mediation struggled — and why

3
UN Special Envoys sent to Syria
0
Peace agreements reached
12/19
UN resolutions vetoed on Syria
  • Unlike West Africa, Syria was caught in a "multipolar trap" — big powers like Russia, the US, and China all had conflicting interests.
  • Russia backed the Assad government; the US backed the opposition. This split the UN Security Council and blocked action.
  • Three UN peace envoys (Annan, Brahimi, de Mistura) tried and failed to broker a ceasefire — not because of bad ideas, but because powerful countries blocked them.
  • Russia and Turkey created their own peace talks ("Astana Talks") outside the UN, competing with and undermining UN legitimacy.
  • Key lesson: UN mediation works best when powerful countries agree to support it. Without that, mediators have no leverage.

Arguments Toolkit

Use these points depending on which team you're on. Make sure you can explain the evidence behind each point!

✅ Positive Team Arguments

  • West Africa proves peacekeeping works — three war-torn countries are now stable democracies
  • 250,000 combatants were disarmed, removing weapons from circulation
  • 3.5 million refugees were able to return home safely
  • UN-backed courts brought war criminals to justice
  • 9 democratic elections were held under UN protection
  • Peacekeeping removes weapons, rebuilds institutions, and restores order

❌ Negative Team Arguments

  • Syria shows the UN can be completely blocked by powerful nations
  • 12 out of 19 Syria resolutions were vetoed — the system is broken
  • Peacekeeping is expensive and takes decades — is it worth the cost?
  • 547 peacekeepers died in the Mano River Basin — a huge human cost
  • Without P5 unity, peace talks fall apart no matter how good the mediators are
  • Alternative peace deals (like Astana) can bypass and undermine UN authority
6

Debate Tips for Success

🎯

Know Your Evidence

Use specific numbers and real examples. "250,000 combatants disarmed" is much stronger than "many soldiers put down their weapons."

👂

Listen Carefully

The best rebuttals come from actually listening to what the other team said. Take notes during their speech!

🗣️

Speak Clearly

Slow down when you make important points. Make eye contact with the Chair. A confident voice makes your argument more convincing.

🤔

Anticipate Counter-Arguments

Before the debate, think: "What will the other side say about MY argument?" Plan your response in advance.

⚖️

Be Fair & Respectful

Challenge ideas, not people. Saying "that argument ignores the evidence" is fine. Personal attacks are never okay.

💡

Solution Minute Prep

Write your solution BEFORE the debate, but be ready to tweak it based on what you heard. A good solution answers the best points from both sides.

Key Vocabulary

Peacekeeping
When international forces are sent to keep the peace in a conflict zone, often after fighting has stopped.
Mediation
When a neutral third party (like the UN) helps two sides in a conflict talk and find a solution.
UN Security Council
The 15-member UN body that authorises peacekeeping missions. The 5 permanent members (P5) can veto any decision.
Veto
The power of P5 countries (US, UK, France, Russia, China) to block any UN Security Council resolution.
Multipolarity
A world where several powerful countries compete for influence, making international agreement harder.
Disarmament
The process of collecting and removing weapons from soldiers or armed groups after a conflict ends.
Motion
The statement or idea being debated — teams are either "for" (positive) or "against" (negative) it.
Rebuttal
Responding to and arguing against a point made by the other team, using your own evidence.
The Chair
The judge of the debate who listens to all arguments and votes on the best solution at the end.
Civil War
A war between groups within the same country, often for control of the government or resources.
Leverage
The power or influence that helps persuade others to agree to a deal or peace agreement.
Internationalised Conflict
A civil war where outside countries get involved by sending weapons, soldiers, or money to one side.
